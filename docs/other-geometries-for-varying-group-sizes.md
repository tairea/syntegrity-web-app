what are the possible geometric shapes that the process still works for varying participant sizes?

Good question — this is where the methodology gets genuinely interesting from a geometry standpoint. Joe Truss did the foundational work here, developing what he called "ShortForms" to extend Beer's original icosahedral protocol to other group sizes. Let me lay out the options.

## The Core Constraint

For the protocol to work, the polyhedron needs three properties:

1. **Regularity** — every edge plays the same role (no person has more or fewer team memberships than anyone else)
2. **Every vertex has the same number of edges meeting at it** (every topic-team is the same size)
3. **An opposite-edge structure** so that critics can be defined symmetrically

The five Platonic solids (tetrahedron, cube, octahedron, dodecahedron, icosahedron) all satisfy property 1 and 2. Some semi-regular (Archimedean) solids do too. Beyond these, you start losing the elegant symmetry that makes Syntegrity work.

## The Canonical Options

Here's a comparison of the polyhedra that have actually been used or seriously proposed for Syntegrity variants:

| Shape | People (edges) | Topics (vertices) | Team size (edges per vertex) | Action plans (faces) | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Tetrahedron** | 6 | 4 | 3 | 4 | Smallest workable form. Reported successful by Truss. |
| **Octahedron** | 12 | 6 | 4 | 8 | Common ShortForm. Good fit for executive teams. |
| **Cube** | 12 | 8 | 3 | 6 | Same edge count as octahedron but different team structure. |
| **Cuboctahedron** | 24 | 12 | 4 | 14 | An Archimedean solid; used in some Truss variants. Bridges to Face Planning. |
| **Icosahedron** | 30 | 12 | 5 | 20 | Beer's canonical form. The gold standard. |
| **Icosidodecahedron** | 60 | 30 | 4 | 32 | Archimedean; experimental, for very large groups. |
| **Super-icosahedron** | 900 (30 × 30) | — | — | — | Theoretical: 30 Syntegrations nested. Discussed by Beer and others. The "World Syntegration" of 1994 was the closest real attempt. |

## What Each Variant Trades Off

**Tetrahedron (6 people, 4 topics).** Every person is on 2 teams as participant and 2 as critic — same role structure as the icosahedron, just at smaller scale. Each team has only 3 members though, which means very intimate but less diverse discussions. Reverberation works almost too well — ideas cycle so fast that the "echo" can become repetitive. Best for tight groups working on a focused issue.

**Octahedron (12 people, 6 topics).** Probably the most useful small-group variant. Each topic-team has 4 members, and the geometry preserves clear opposite-edge symmetry for critics. Allenna Leonard's article notes that **meetings of 24 people have proven effective with similar outcomes to full syntegrations** — and the octahedron at 12 is essentially half of that. A 2-day event works comfortably.

**Cube (12 people, 8 topics).** Same person count as octahedron but more topics with smaller teams (3 per team). Good when you have many distinct sub-issues to cover but not many people. Less commonly used because team sizes of 3 don't leave room for much disagreement.

**Cuboctahedron (24 people, 12 topics).** This is a clever Archimedean solid that gives you the same 12 topics as the icosahedron but with smaller teams of 4 (instead of 5) and only 24 people. It's the closest thing to a "full" Syntegration that's still scalable down. Useful when you want the topic richness of the canonical form but can only muster 24 participants.

**Icosahedron (30 people, 12 topics).** Beer's original. Five-person teams are large enough to surface real disagreement, small enough to converge. Twelve topics are enough to cover most complex issues without overwhelming. Every person has 4 team involvements. Twenty triangular faces give you a natural action-planning structure (Face Planning). This is the form everything else is measured against.

**Icosidodecahedron (60 people, 30 topics).** Experimental territory. With 60 people you can cover a lot of perspectives, but coordination becomes a logistical challenge — you'd need 30 simultaneous team rooms, far more facilitators, and a much longer event (probably 5+ days). The advantage is that you can handle truly large stakeholder groups (a whole company division, a multi-organisation coalition) in a single Syntegration rather than rolling out multiple icosahedra.

**Super-icosahedron / nested Syntegrations.** Beer himself was interested in this — running 30 Syntegrations and then nesting their outputs in a meta-Syntegration of 30 representatives, one from each. The 1994 World Syntegration was the only real attempt: 30 cities held simultaneous local Syntegrations, then one representative from each came together. This is the theoretical upper bound.

## What Doesn't Work (and Why)

You can't just pick any polyhedron and run a Syntegration on it. Some shapes look promising but break the geometry:

- **Dodecahedron (30 edges, 20 vertices)** — same edge count as the icosahedron, but each vertex has only 3 edges meeting it, so teams would be only 3 people. The opposite-edge property also doesn't map cleanly. Beer specifically rejected it in favour of the icosahedron.
- **Triangular prism, pentagonal prism, etc.** — non-regular polyhedra mean people would have unequal roles. Breaks the democratic principle.
- **Hand-tailored shapes for arbitrary numbers (e.g., a pentagonal bipyramid for 15 people)** — possible in principle, but you lose the regularity that produces fair role distribution. Probably gives you less than the corresponding regular form. Better to add or drop a couple of people to fit a Platonic solid than to invent custom geometry.

## Truss's Insight: Why Geometry Matters

Joe Truss's contribution to the methodology was recognising that the geometry isn't just a metaphor — it's the source of Syntegrity's properties. The Coherent Architecture paper makes the point that **the regularity of the polyhedron is what produces equal voice, balanced reverberation, and fair critic distribution.** Lose the regularity and you lose the methodology's distinctive value.

This is why most practitioners stick to the Platonic and a few Archimedean solids. The temptation to invent a custom shape for whatever group size you happen to have is usually a mistake — better to use the nearest regular form and adapt the participant count to fit it.

## Practical Recommendation by Group Size

| Group size | Recommended shape | Trade-offs |
|---|---|---|
| 6 | Tetrahedron | Workable but small; consider whether you need Syntegrity at all |
| 8–10 | Pad to 12, use octahedron | Two extra participants give you proper team sizes |
| 12 | Octahedron | Sweet spot for small organisations |
| 13–20 | Pad to 24, use cuboctahedron | Or drop to octahedron if recruitment is hard |
| 24 | Cuboctahedron | Closest to a full Syntegration at smaller scale |
| 25–35 | Trim or pad to 30, use icosahedron | The canonical experience |
| 35–55 | Run icosahedron with 30; either roll out a second event or use Face Planning to extend |
| 56+ | Icosidodecahedron, or multiple parallel Syntegrations with nested follow-up |

The general rule: **fit the group to the shape, not the shape to the group.** A well-run octahedron with 12 people will produce better outcomes than a hand-tailored 10-person bespoke polyhedron, even though the latter sounds more accommodating.
